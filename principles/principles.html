1. Balance

Balance is the distribution of visual weight in a composition. Visual weight is determined by size, color, value, and texture.

  • Symmetrical: Elements are mirrored along a central axis. Creates stability, formality, and order.
  • Asymmetrical: Elements are unequal but balanced by scale or contrast. Creates dynamism, energy, and visual interest.
  • Radial: Elements radiate from a circular focal point.

2. Contrast

Contrast is the arrangement of opposite elements (light vs. dark, rough vs. smooth, large vs. small) to make a specific element stand out and create visual interest.

  • Contrast guides attention by showing differences.
  • Lack of contrast leads to flat, boring designs.
  • Essential for visual readability and accessibility.

3. Alignment

Alignment connects elements together on an invisible axis. It establishes order, cleanliness, and professionalism, preventing layouts from looking scattered or random.

  • Left Alignment: Traditional, highly readable for body text.
  • Center Alignment: Formal, great for titles and certificates.
  • Right Alignment: Creative, used for side panels or numeric charts.

4. Hierarchy

Hierarchy establishes a clear reading order. By altering scale, font weight, positioning, and color, you dictate which visual element is seen first, second, and last.

  • Directs the viewer’s scanning flow.
  • Highlights CTA (Call to Action) buttons and headings.
  • Groups information structurally.

5. Repetition

Repetition utilizes consistent design elements (colors, fonts, shapes, sizes) throughout a composition to tie separate parts together and build unity.

  • Creates visual texture and rhythm.
  • Builds identity and consistent brand guidelines.
  • Increases predictability and comfort for user interfaces.

6. Proximity

Proximity states that elements placed close together are perceived as sharing a relationship, while elements placed far apart are perceived as disconnected.

  • Simplifies layouts and organizes information.
  • Reduces clutter by grouping related cards or menu headers.
  • Establishes structure for forms and cards.

7. White Space

White Space (Negative Space) is the empty area surrounding active visual components. It is the breathing room that prevents visual overload and guides focus.

  • Improves readability of body copy.
  • Surrounding an element with negative space elevates its importance.
  • Creates high-end, premium layouts (e.g. Apple minimalist branding).

8. Proportion

Proportion (Scale) is the size relationship between elements. It can be used to emphasize key features, suggest depth, or build visual excitement.

  • Shows hierarchy: Larger elements command primary attention.
  • Suggests scale: Large mountains with a tiny hiker silhouette.
  • Unexpected proportions spark immediate curiosity.

9. Movement

Movement guides the viewer's path of sight. By using leading lines, curves, gradients, and arrows, you dictate how a student scans your composition.

  • Directs eye flow from heading to body to button.
  • Simulates dynamic motion or speed on a static canvas.
  • Uses diagonals and curves to break visual grids.

Visual Path Flow

10. Rhythm

Rhythm is visual tempo. It is built by repeating elements in a pattern with structured intervals, establishing a sense of movement.

  • Regular: Intervals are identical (like standard columns).
  • Flowing: Follows curvy, smooth waves.
  • Progressive: Gradual scaling or shade changes over intervals, implying growth.

11. Unity

Unity (Harmony) measures how well all design components fit together. Visual elements should feel connected and part of a whole, rather than scattered assets.

  • Established by limited color schemes and matching margins.
  • Aligning shapes and fonts creates structured harmony.
  • "Variety without unity is chaotic; unity without variety is boring."

12. Variety

Variety introduces differences in color, shape, scale, or texture to capture attention and prevent a unified composition from looking monotone or lifeless.

  • Complements unity to keep layouts interesting.
  • Highlights anomalies: A red block in a grid of grey blocks.
  • Sparks interest and stops passive scanning.

Visual Interest Variety

13. Pattern

Pattern is the systematic, repeated replication of design components (motifs). It establishes textures and structural motifs in layouts.

  • Forms repeating decorative backdrops.
  • Can structure complex data visualizations.
  • Used to create depth and tactile backgrounds.

14. Emphasis

Emphasis makes a specific element the focal point. It commands attention by creating high contrast in scale, color, positioning, or shape.

  • Ensures key messages are read immediately.
  • Established via color isolation or scaling.
  • "If everything is emphasized, nothing is emphasized."

15. Simplicity

Simplicity reduces design components to their functional essence. Removing visual noise ensures clean, immediate communication of the core message.

  • Minimalism: Keeps only features critical to communication.
  • Eliminates unnecessary lines, boxes, patterns, and icons.
  • Directs user actions immediately and cuts cognitive load.
