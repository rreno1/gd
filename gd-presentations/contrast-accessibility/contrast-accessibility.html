02

What is Accessibility (a11y)?

Accessibility is the practice of making your designs and digital products usable by everyone—including people with visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive disabilities.

  • Inclusive Design: Designing for disabled users creates a better experience for everyone (e.g. high contrast benefits a user reading in direct sunlight).
  • WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines): The global technical standard governing digital accessibility, categorizing rules into AA (standard) and AAA (extreme) ratings.
  • In IT, digital accessibility is often a legal requirement, not just a design preference.
👁️

Perceivable

Users must be able to comprehend the info (e.g. providing alt-text for images).

🕹️

Operable

Interface cannot require actions a user cannot perform (e.g. keyboard navigation).

🧠

Understandable

Information and layout operation must be logical and readable.

🛡️

Robust

Content must remain compatible with assistive technologies (like screen readers).

04

WCAG Contrast Standards

WCAG defines mathematical equations to calculate color contrast ratios between text foregrounds and background backdrops.

  • Normal Text (under 18pt): Minimum contrast ratio of **4.5:1** for AA pass; **7.0:1** for AAA.
  • Large Text (over 18pt or bold): Minimum contrast ratio of **3.0:1** for AA pass; **4.5:1** for AAA.
  • Contrast calculations are based on relative luminance (perceived brightness) of colors.

05

Color Vision Deficiencies (CVD)

Roughly 300 million people live with color blindness. Designers must structure interfaces so color choice is never the sole indicator of actions.

  • Protanopia (Red-blind): Struggles to see red light spectrum. Red warning badges look brownish/grey.
  • Deuteranopia (Green-blind): Struggles to see green spectrum. Most common; red and green alerts merge.
  • Tritanopia (Blue-blind): Rare; struggles to tell blues from greens, and yellows from violet.

06

Communicating Beyond Color

If a user cannot distinguish red from green, status indicators relying purely on color will fail completely.

  • The Rule: Always support colors with **icons, explicit text labels, underline weights, or patterns**.
  • This prevents color vision deficiencies from blocking critical actions.

07

Legibility & Reading Layouts

Accessibility also encompasses visual reading ease, layout density, and typographic structure rules.

16px

Minimum Body Size

Text blocks in digital layout formats should maintain at least 16px font height to prevent reading squint.

1.5

Line Spacing (Leading)

Line spacing should be set to 1.5 times the font size, allowing empty tracks for visual comfort.

80ch

Maximum Column Width

Limit paragraph widths to 80 characters (or less) per row line to prevent eye tracking fatigue.

200%

Text Resizing Zoom

Ensure text layout does not break or overlap when browser scaling zoom increases up to 200%.

08

Interactive Targets & Focus States

Interactive layouts must support keyboard-only navigators and touchscreen clicks accuracy.

  • Focus Outlines: Never hide keyboard focus rings. Users who navigate with 'Tab' keys require highlighted outlines to see active selections.
  • Touch Targets (WCAG 2.2): Interactive targets (buttons, links) must span at least **24x24px** (preferably Apple's **44x44px** standard) to prevent missed taps.

09

Alt-Text & Screen Readers

Screen readers read digital content aloud for blind users. Alternative descriptions (Alt-Text) must describe the content and purpose of images.

  • Contextual: Focus on explaining what the image represents, not just raw details.
  • Avoid redundant text: Never start alt-text with "image of..." or "picture of...". Screen readers announce the image tag automatically.
  • Decorative graphics (shapes, dividers) should have an empty `alt=""` tag.
